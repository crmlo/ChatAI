07 · Design Principles
Design Principles
The principles behind
every decision I made.
01
Trust is the product
From: interviews with healthcare professionals
In high-stakes contexts, a technically perfect agent fails if people don't trust it. Designing for trust isn't a feature — it's the foundation.
02
Disruption never reaches the patient
From: exception flow definition
Failures, latency, and handoffs happen on the professional's interface. The patient never experiences a break — their journey stays continuous.
03
Design for who approves, not just who uses
From: understanding the coordinator's role
AI adoption in organizations depends on the people with accountability. The audit tab wasn't a product feature — it was change management.
04
Agent transparency as a right
From: tension between autonomy and control
Professionals always know when the agent is on, what it's doing, and can take over at any moment. Visibility is non-negotiable.
05
Failure deserves the same care as the happy path
From: exception flow mapping
In healthcare, failure moments are where the product proves — or loses — its reliability. Every error scenario has a specific, intentional behavior.

09 · Key Decisions
Key Decisions
The hardest calls —
and why I made them.
Agent visibility
Showing when the agent is responding could create friction and feel impersonal for the patient
→ Full transparency for the professional, seamless experience for the patient
The professional always knows. The patient never notices the seam.
Audit — mandatory or optional?
Mandatory creates overhead. Optional might not be used — and without it, coordinators won't approve AI
→ Available and accessible, not mandatory — but designed to be irresistible
Trust is built through visibility. The audit tab became the key driver of adoption.
Where failure happens
Showing errors to patients is transparent, but in healthcare it can cause unnecessary anxiety
→ Disruption happens on the professional's interface — never on the patient's
The professional gets the alert and takes over. The patient never feels the break.
Who defines the guardrails
Letting the client configure everything is flexible, but creates real risk in sensitive clinical situations
→ Product defines the non-negotiable minimum; client configures within those limits
Human life comes before operational efficiency — and that had to be coded into the product.

13 · Exception flows
Exception flows
Designing for failure with the same
care as the happy path.
ScenarioWhat the patient seesWhat the professional seesSystem action
Warning
Agent latency
Takes longer than expected to respond
 No changeConversation stays open. No error shown — no reason to add anxiety in a healthcare context. Alert in the interface"The agent is taking longer than expected." Take-over button highlighted. Immediate signalConversation flagged. Take-over control enabled with visual priority.
Critical
LLM downtime
API unavailable — agent can't respond
 No changePatient is not informed about the technical failure. Alert across all conversationsEvery conversation assigned to the agent gets flagged. Queue prioritized automatically. Immediate fallbackAgent removed. Conversations redistributed to available professionals or waitlist.
Core principle: the patient never perceives a technical failure. The disruption happens on the professional's interface — never on the patient's experience.
14 · The audit tab
The audit tab
Institutional trust
is a design problem.
video: coordinator navigating the audit tab
Why it exists
Coordinators wouldn't approve AI for their clients without visibility. They're accountable to the healthcare operators — and that accountability had to be respected.
What it solves
A structured log of everything the agent did. Reviewable conversation by conversation, with performance ratings and consolidated history.
The client buys Clara — not "an AI agent". An assistant with a name, her own NPS, and a full auditable history.

16 · Learnings
Learnings
What this project changed
about how I think about design.
01
In AI products, trust is the product.
You can have the best model in the world. If people don't trust it, the product doesn't exist. Designing for trust isn't a feature — it's the foundation everything else is built on.
02
Designing for AI means designing for failure.
Exception flows aren't edge cases — they're where the product proves it's reliable. Latency, human handoff, and model errors deserve the same design care as the happy path.
03
AI adoption is an organizational problem.
The audit tab wasn't a product feature — it was change management. You design for the end user, but also for the person who has to say yes to putting AI in front of patients.
AI in my process
I used Figma Make for quick interface validation instead of building full prototypes. Claude and GPT for research synthesis, interview scripts, and documentation. I'm currently learning Cursor + Figma MCP to get design closer to engineering. This project also taught me something more fundamental: the difference between using AI as a tool and designing for AI as a material — trust, fallbacks, feedback loops, and transparency are design problems, not just engineering ones.
